STAKEHOLDER EVIDENCE - METHODS & ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY
Document Created: December 7, 2025
Project: Manager Guidance Consistency Impact on Employee Engagement

===========================================================================

RESEARCH QUESTION
How do stakeholders perceive and respond to inconsistent manager guidance in hybrid work environments?

===========================================================================

STAKEHOLDER EVIDENCE APPROACH: SECONDARY SOURCES ANALYSIS
This stakeholder evidence uses ONLY secondary sources - no primary data collection was conducted.
All evidence comes from published surveys, reports, and verified secondary data sources.

===========================================================================

WHY STAKEHOLDER EVIDENCE MATTERS (CEBMa Module 11)
Stakeholder perceptions are "social facts" with actual effects on decision outcomes.
Stakeholders with POWER can block decisions or impede implementation.

Two types of impact assessed:
1. PRACTICAL IMPACT: Can stakeholders influence/block decisions about manager training investments?
2. ETHICAL IMPACT: Who is harmed by inconsistent manager guidance?

===========================================================================

STAKEHOLDER MAP: POWER/INTEREST FRAMEWORK

HIGH POWER, HIGH INTEREST (Manage Closely):
- C-Suite Executives (CEOs, CHROs): Control training budgets, set organizational development priorities
- Senior HR Leaders: Design/implement manager training programs, allocate L&D budgets
- Business Unit Leaders: Oversee multiple manager teams, influence training adoption
- Finance Leaders (CFOs): Approve training investments, require ROI justification

HIGH POWER, LOW INTEREST (Keep Satisfied):
- Board of Directors: Governance oversight, focus on financial performance not operational training
- Legal/Compliance: Concerned with risk mitigation (harassment, discrimination), less focused on engagement
- IT Leadership: Enable technology for feedback systems, limited interest in manager soft skills

LOW POWER, HIGH INTEREST (Keep Informed):
- Mid-Level Managers: Directly affected by training requirements, need guidance on feedback delivery
- Frontline Employees: Affected by manager guidance quality, limited decision-making power
- HR Business Partners: Support manager development, advocate for training but don't control budgets

LOW POWER, LOW INTEREST (Monitor):
- External Consultants: Offer training solutions but don't control adoption decisions
- Industry Associations (SHRM, ATD): Provide best practice guidance, limited organizational influence
- General Public/Job Seekers: May consider manager quality in employer reputation, minimal direct power

DATA SOURCES USED (SECONDARY SOURCES ONLY)

SOURCE 1: Gallup State of the American Workplace Reports (2013-2024)
URL: https://www.gallup.com/workplace/
Data Type: National employee engagement surveys (thousands of U.S. workers annually)
Stakeholder Groups Represented:
- Frontline employees (engagement, feedback experiences)
- Managers (indirectly - via employee reports of manager behaviors)
Survey Design: Gallup Q12 employee engagement survey, nationally representative samples
Topics Assessed: Manager impact on engagement, feedback frequency effects, remote/hybrid work patterns
Key Findings for Stakeholder Evidence:
- 70% of engagement variance attributable to managers (validates manager centrality)
- 51% of employees "not engaged" + 16% "actively disengaged" = TWO-THIRDS affected by poor management
- Remote/hybrid employees 31% engaged vs. on-site 34% (3-point gap suggests manager adaptation issues)

SOURCE 2: Gallup Practitioner Interviews - Reframed as Stakeholder Evidence
Original Purpose: Practitioner evidence (Module 4)
Reframed For: Stakeholder evidence (Module 11)
Sources: Dr. Jim Harter (Chief Scientist), Denise McLain (VP Client Success), Bailey Nelson (Learning Architect)
Stakeholder Groups: HR LEADERS and ORGANIZATIONAL DEVELOPMENT PROFESSIONALS
- HIGH POWER (design training programs, advise C-suite)
- HIGH INTEREST (career success tied to engagement outcomes)
Number: 3 Gallup practitioners with combined 60+ years experience advising 1,000+ organizations
Method: Published Gallup articles, podcasts, and LinkedIn posts analyzed for stakeholder concerns, implementation barriers, and organizational readiness perspectives

SOURCE 3: LinkedIn Workforce Report - Remote Work Trends (2020-2024)
URL: https://economicgraph.linkedin.com/
Data Type: Aggregated job posting and workforce behavior data
Stakeholder Group Represented: EMPLOYERS and EMPLOYEES (behavioral evidence)
Finding: 16% of jobs remote-capable in 2020 → 20% by 2024 (hybrid work normalization)
Relevance: Validates stakeholder context - managers operating in NEW hybrid environment

SOURCE 4: Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) - Training Investment Surveys
URL: https://www.shrm.org/
Data Type: Annual HR benchmarking surveys (thousands of HR professionals)
Stakeholder Groups Represented:
- HR Leaders (training budget decision-makers)
- Finance Leaders (indirectly - via reported budget constraints)
Topics Assessed: Training spend per employee, manager development priorities, hybrid work adaptation challenges
Key Findings for Stakeholder Evidence:
- Average $1,308 training spend per employee (2023) - establishes budget baseline for manager training ROI discussions
- 68% of organizations increased manager training post-pandemic (validates stakeholder awareness of problem)
- Top training priority: "Leadership and management development" (confirms HIGH INTEREST from HR stakeholders)

ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS

✅ Informed Consent: All secondary sources were publicly available; no primary data collection requiring consent
✅ Confidentiality: Gallup survey respondents were anonymous; practitioner interviews were public statements
✅ No Harm: Secondary analysis only; no direct stakeholder contact
⚠️ Bias Acknowledgment: Selection bias present - over-representation of HIGH POWER stakeholders (HR leaders, executives)
⚠️ Employee Voice: Employees represented through aggregated survey data, not direct qualitative interviews (may miss nuanced experiences)

===========================================================================

LIMITATIONS OF STAKEHOLDER EVIDENCE APPROACH

1. SECONDARY SOURCES ONLY: No primary stakeholder interviews conducted
   - Cannot explore organization-specific contexts or unique stakeholder concerns
   - Limited ability to probe implementation barriers or political dynamics

2. SELECTION BIAS: Over-representation of HIGH POWER stakeholders (HR leaders, executives)
   - HR professionals (Gallup practitioners, SHRM surveys) well-represented
   - C-suite perspectives inferred from financial data, not direct statements
   - Mid-level managers under-represented (only indirect evidence via employee reports)

3. UNDER-REPRESENTATION: Managers lack direct voice
   - Employees report manager behaviors (feedback frequency, guidance quality)
   - But managers' own perspectives on barriers/challenges not directly captured
   - Cannot assess manager READINESS or WILLINGNESS to change feedback practices

4. TIMING: Gallup data spans 2013-2024 (includes pre-pandemic, pandemic, hybrid transition)
   - Captures longitudinal trends BUT specific 2024 context (hybrid work normalization) most relevant
   - SHRM training surveys 2023 data - recent but may lag current 2025 priorities

5. SELF-REPORT: Survey responses subject to social desirability bias
   - Employees may over-report engagement (want to appear positive)
   - HR leaders may over-report training investments (want to appear competent)

6. AGGREGATED DATA: National averages may obscure industry/organization differences
   - Gallup reports national 33% engagement - but tech vs. manufacturing vs. healthcare may differ
   - SHRM training spend $1,308 average - but varies by organization size, industry, profitability

7. NO BEHAVIORAL VALIDATION: Except turnover data
   - Turnover (18-43% differences) validates employee responses to poor management
   - But no direct observation of manager feedback practices or training effectiveness

WHO WE HAVE:

✅ Frontline employees (via Gallup surveys) - STRONG representation (large sample, national scope)
✅ Internal organizational stakeholders - ADEQUATE representation
✅ Multiple industries (Gallup 54 industries, 96 countries) - EXCELLENT representation

WHO WE'RE MISSING:
❌ Mid-level managers (direct voice) - WEAK representation (only indirect via employee reports)
❌ Business unit leaders - NO representation
❌ Board of Directors - NO representation
❌ Finance leaders (direct voice) - WEAK representation (only indirect via business case)
❌ Organization-specific contexts - NO representation (only aggregated national data)

CONFIDENCE LEVEL: MEDIUM-HIGH

✅ Representative of EMPLOYEE EXPERIENCES: STRONG
   - Gallup surveys thousands of employees annually
   - 2.7M employees in meta-analysis (456 studies)
   - Engagement and feedback frequency experiences well-documented

✅ Representative of HR LEADER PERSPECTIVES: STRONG
   - Gallup practitioners (Harter, McLain, Nelson) provide direct voice
   - SHRM surveys capture HR professional priorities and budgets
   - Training investment patterns documented

⚠️ Representative of MANAGER PERSPECTIVES: WEAK
   - Managers described as finding feedback "intimidating" but no direct survey of managers
   - Cannot assess manager readiness, confidence, or implementation barriers directly

⚠️ Representative of C-SUITE DECISION-MAKERS: MODERATE
   - Financial case well-documented ($450-550B cost, 23% profitability, 18% productivity)
   - But no direct CEO/CHRO survey responses on training investment decisions
   - Cannot assess budget allocation trade-offs or competing priorities

❌ Representative of ORGANIZATIONAL POLITICS: WEAK
   - No evidence on internal resistance, power dynamics, or implementation barriers
   - Cannot assess who might BLOCK manager training initiatives or why

Overall Stakeholder Landscape: PARTIALLY REPRESENTATIVE
- STRONG for employee experiences and HR leader perspectives
- WEAK for manager direct voice and C-suite decision-making processes
- ABSENT for organizational implementation dynamics and political barriers
